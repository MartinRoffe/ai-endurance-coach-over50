How the week runs

Three appliances, one short Sunday prep session, and a clean split between what you batch-cook and what arrives ready. To stop it getting repetitive, breakfasts and lunches run on a two-week A/B rotation — Week A one week, Week B the next, then back to A. Each week is still just two breakfasts and two lunches, keeping the Sunday session to roughly 60–75 minutes. Mon–Thu evening meals are Gousto; weekends are cooked fresh around your rides and stay the same each week.

Both weeks share the same weekend: Sat & Sun start with a pre-ride fix, then a post-ride recovery bowl, with fresh griddle dinners Fri–Sun. The weekend plan and the on-bike fuel don't change — only weekday breakfasts and lunches alternate.

Protein first

Over 50 you need a higher leucine threshold to trigger muscle synthesis. 30–40g every meal, repeated through the day — distribution beats total.

Carbs follow training

Generous on weekend ride days, moderate midweek, lower on rest days. Carbs are a tool, not the enemy of fat loss.

Deficit at the table

The modest calorie deficit comes from portion control at meals — never from under-fuelling rides, which costs muscle, not fat.

Two weeks, still simple

Alternating A and B keeps it interesting, but each week is still just two breakfasts, two lunches, big-batched. Same appliances, same ~60–75 min prep.

04

The Sunday session

Down to roughly 60–75 minutes, most of it unattended, in both weeks. The timeline below is Week A; Week B follows the exact same rhythm — start the unattended appliances, do one or two protein cooks on the griddle, bake or assemble the breakfast, then portion. The Recipes section has full methods for every dish in both weeks.

AWeek A — Sunday session
0:00 · Start the machines

Rice cooker + soup maker on

Big batch of rice in the cooker. Chopped veg, lentils and stock into the soup maker on its full cycle. Both now run unattended — this is where the time saving lives.

0:05 · One protein cook

Griddle the chicken — all of it

Cook the whole batch of chicken in one go on the Blackstone. Some goes into the rice bowls, the rest gets shredded into the soup. One cook feeds both lunches.

0:15 · Oven tray

Egg muffins in

Whisk 10 eggs, fold in the fillings, pour into the tray, oven on. Walk away for 20–25 min — set a timer and use the gap for the next step.

0:20 · No-cook assembly

Build the oats jars

While everything cooks: spoon oats, protein, yoghurt, chia, milk and berries into three jars. Hard-boil a few eggs for the soup sides if you want them.

0:40 · Portion & store

Into containers, done

Rice + chicken + veg into three bowls; soup into two tubs; muffins out of the oven to cool. Fridge the week, freeze anything for Thu/Fri. Tidy up and you're finished.

The big lever: two recipes per week means far fewer separate operations, and in Week A cooking chicken once for both lunches removes a whole step. If even this feels long, the Skyr-pot breakfast and a shop-bought soup are zero-prep get-outs that still hit your protein targets.
BWeek B — Sunday session
0:00 · Start the machines

Rice cooker on + pasta water heating

Rice for the chilli goes in the cooker (unattended). Put a pan of water on to boil for the pasta. Boil 4 eggs at the same time for the yoghurt-pot breakfasts.

0:05 · Brown the mince

Beef chilli on the griddle

Brown the beef mince on the Blackstone, then add onion, pepper, sweet potato, beans, tomatoes and spices and let it simmer. This is the one dish that needs a little stirring.

0:10 · Cook pasta + chicken

Pasta in, chicken on

Pasta into the boiling water (cook per packet, then drain and cool). Griddle the chicken for the pasta pots alongside the chilli — both fit on the Blackstone.

0:20 · Bake the oat bars

Banana oat bars in the oven

Mash bananas, mix with oats, eggs, protein, milk and honey, pour into a lined tray, and bake. Walk away for 20–25 min.

0:40 · Portion & store

Into containers, done

Chilli + rice into three tubs; pasta + pesto + chicken into two pots; oat bars out to cool and slice; yoghurt pots and boiled eggs ready. Fridge the week, freeze any Thu/Fri portions.

05

Step-by-step recipes

Every recipe written out in full — no steps skipped, no cooking knowledge assumed. If a cooking word is unfamiliar, the glossary just below explains the ones used here. The orange-edged notes are food-safety steps that genuinely matter — especially for chicken and rice — so don't skip those.

Cooking terms used on this page

Dice
Cut into small cubes, roughly 1cm — about the size of the tip of your little finger. It doesn't need to be neat.
Finely chop
Cut into small pieces, smaller than a dice. Again, neatness doesn't matter — smaller just means it cooks faster and spreads through the dish.
Simmer
Liquid heated until small bubbles rise gently — one notch below a full rolling boil. If it's bubbling violently, turn the heat down a little.
Drain
Tip the food into a colander (the bowl with holes) in the sink to let water run off.
Season
Add salt and pepper. "Season to taste" means add a little, try it, add more if it needs it.
Preheat
Turn the oven on ahead of time so it's already hot when the food goes in. Takes about 10 minutes.
Medium heat
On a hob dial of 1–9, that's about 5–6. Hot enough to cook and sizzle, not so hot things burn.
Piping hot
Heated all the way through until steaming — the test for safely reheated food.
AWeek A recipes
Breakfast · makes 3 jars (Mon / Wed / Fri)

High-Protein Overnight Oats

No cooking at allHands-on: ~8 minNeeds: 3 jars or tubs with lids

What goes in each jar

  • 60 gPorridge oatsAbout 6 heaped tablespoons if you don't have scales
  • 1 scoopProtein powder (whey or casein)Use the scoop that comes in the tub — usually ~30g
  • 200 mlMilkAbout a large glass; any milk works
  • 100 gGreek yoghurtAbout 4 tablespoons
  • 1 tbspChia seeds (tbsp = tablespoon)
  • 100 gBerriesA small handful; frozen is fine and cheaper
  • 1 tspHoney (tsp = teaspoon)

Method

  1. Put the oats, protein powder and chia seeds into a jar. Give it a quick stir with a spoon so the powder is mixed through the oats.
  2. Pour in the milk, add the Greek yoghurt and the honey. Stir well for about 15 seconds until there are no dry patches and the powder has dissolved. It will look quite runny — that's correct, the oats soak it up overnight.
  3. If you're using fresh berries, you can stir them in now. If using frozen, it's better to leave them in a separate small bag and add them in the morning so they don't make everything watery.Tip: for the freshest result, add any berries on the morning you eat it rather than mixing through on Sunday.
  4. Put the lid on and put it in the fridge. Repeat for the other two jars. That's it — no cooking. They're ready to eat cold straight from the fridge the next morning, and keep for 4–5 days.
Breakfast · makes 12 muffins (Tue / Thu + spares)

Egg & Vegetable Muffins

Oven does the workHands-on: ~15 minOven: 20–25 min

Kit you'll need

12-hole muffin trayPaper or silicone muffin cases1 large mixing bowl1 frying panA fork or whisk

Ingredients

  • 10Eggs
  • 200 gSmoked bacon (or cooked ham)About 6 rashers of bacon, or 1 small pack of ham
  • 100 gPotato, already cookedAbout 1 medium potato — see step 1
  • 1Red pepper
  • 2 handfulsSpinach
  • A few pinchesSalt, pepper and paprika
  • 1 tspOil for the pan (any cooking oil)

Method

  1. Cook the potato first (if it's not already cooked). Peel 1 medium potato, cut it into roughly 1cm cubes, put them in a small pan, cover with water, and boil for about 10 minutes until a fork slides in easily. Drain and set aside.Shortcut: a handful of frozen diced potato or frozen hash browns, cooked per the bag, saves this step entirely.
  2. Preheat the oven to 180°C (fan) / 200°C (no fan) / gas mark 6. Pop 12 paper or silicone cases into the muffin tray.
  3. Dice the bacon into small pieces. Heat the 1 tsp oil in the frying pan on medium heat, add the bacon, and cook for about 4–5 minutes, stirring now and then, until it's cooked through and lightly golden. Tip it onto a plate to cool slightly. (If using ready-cooked ham, just dice it — no frying needed.)
  4. Cut the red pepper: slice around the stalk, pull out the seedy core and throw it away, then dice the rest into small pieces.
  5. Crack all 10 eggs into the large bowl. Beat them with a fork or whisk for about 30 seconds until the yellows and whites are fully combined and slightly frothy. Add a few pinches of salt, pepper and paprika.
  6. Add the diced potato, bacon, pepper and spinach to the bowl. (The spinach looks like a lot but shrinks right down when it cooks.) Stir everything together.
  7. Spoon the mixture into the 12 cases, filling each about three-quarters full. A ladle or a jug makes this easier and tidier.
  8. Bake for 20–25 minutes. They're done when the tops are set, slightly risen and golden, and don't wobble when you gently shake the tray.Safety check: egg should be fully set with no runny liquid in the middle. If unsure, push a knife into one — it should come out clean, not wet.
  9. Let them cool in the tray for 10 minutes, then lift them out. Once fully cold, store in a tub in the fridge (4 days) or freeze. Eat 2–3 per breakfast, cold or warmed in a microwave for about 40 seconds.

Chicken & Rice Bowls

Rice cooker + griddle/panHands-on: ~20 minCook extra chicken for the soup

Kit you'll need

Rice cookerBlackstone griddle or a frying panChopping board + knife3 lunch containers with lids

Ingredients

  • 1.2 kgChicken breastThis makes extra on purpose — about half goes into the soup recipe below
  • 250 gRice, uncooked (basmati or long-grain)About 1½ rice-cooker cups; this is the right amount for 3 bowls
  • 1 largeHead of broccoli
  • 2 handfulsFrozen edamame (soya beans)
  • 2Carrots
  • 3 tbspSoy sauce
  • 1 tbspSesame oil (or any cooking oil)
  • 1 tsp eachGinger and garlicJars of pre-chopped ginger and garlic are perfect and save effort

Method

  1. Start the rice. Measure 250g (about 1½ cups) of rice into the rice cooker. Rinse it under cold water in a sieve first if you have one — it stops it being sticky, but it's optional. Add water to the line the rice cooker shows for that amount of rice (or roughly 1½ times the depth of the rice), put the lid on and switch it on. It turns itself off when done — leave it alone.
  2. Prepare the chicken. Put the chicken breasts on a chopping board. Cut each one into strips about 2cm wide, then across into bite-sized chunks.Safety: raw chicken — wash your hands, the board and the knife with hot soapy water straight after, and don't let raw chicken touch anything you'll eat without cooking (like the salad or cooked rice).
  3. Cook the chicken. Heat the sesame oil on the griddle or in a frying pan on medium-high heat. Add the chicken chunks in a single layer. Cook for about 8–10 minutes, turning every couple of minutes, until every piece is white all the way through with no pink, and the outsides are lightly golden.Safety check: cut the biggest piece in half — the inside must be white/opaque, never pink or glassy. If in doubt, cook another 2 minutes. This is the one step worth being fussy about.
  4. About halfway through, add the ginger and garlic and the soy sauce to the pan and stir so it coats the chicken.
  5. Cook the vegetables. While the chicken cooks: cut the broccoli into small florets (bite-sized trees). Peel and grate the carrots, or cut into thin sticks. Put the broccoli and the frozen edamame in a microwave bowl with a splash of water, cover with a plate, and microwave for about 4 minutes until the broccoli is bright green and just tender. (Or use the rice cooker's steamer basket if it has one.)
  6. Split the chicken. Once cooked, set about half the chicken aside in a separate tub for the soup recipe (it'll go in the fridge). The other half stays for these bowls.
  7. Build the 3 bowls. Divide the rice between 3 containers. Top each with chicken, broccoli, edamame and carrot. Let everything cool.Important for rice: get the bowls into the fridge within 1 hour of cooking. Cooked rice left out too long can cause food poisoning. Cooling it quickly and refrigerating promptly is what keeps it safe.
  8. Lids on, into the fridge. Reheat in the microwave until piping hot (steaming all the way through), about 2–3 minutes. Eats well for 3–4 days — see the storage note about Friday's bowl.

Lentil & Chicken Soup

Soup maker does it allHands-on: ~10 minUses chicken from the bowls above

Kit you'll need

Soup makerChopping board + knife2 soup containers with lids

Ingredients

  • 100 gRed lentils, driedAbout 1 mug; no soaking needed, they cook in the soup
  • 1Onion
  • 2Carrots
  • 1 stickCelery (optional)
  • 1 smallSweet potato
  • 1Stock cube (chicken or vegetable) + boiling water
  • Half theCooked chicken set aside from the bowls recipe
  • 1 tspCumin and smoked paprika (optional, for flavour)

Method

  1. Chop the vegetables roughly — they get blended at the end, so neatness genuinely doesn't matter. Peel the onion and cut into rough chunks. Peel the carrots and sweet potato and cut into rough 2cm chunks. Slice the celery if using.
  2. Rinse the lentils in a sieve under the cold tap for a few seconds to wash off any dust.
  3. Put it all in the soup maker: the chopped vegetables, the rinsed lentils, and the spices if using. Do not add the chicken yet.
  4. Make the stock: dissolve 1 stock cube in boiling water (follow the cube's packet — usually 1 cube to ~500ml). Pour it into the soup maker up to its maximum fill line — never above it, or it can overflow.
  5. Choose the "smooth" setting and press start. The soup maker now heats and blends everything by itself — this takes around 25 minutes. You don't need to watch it.If your soup maker has no blend button: let it finish the heat cycle, then blend with a hand blender, or leave it chunky — both are fine.
  6. When it's finished, stir in the cooked chicken from the tub. The heat of the soup warms it through in a minute or two. Taste, and add a pinch of salt if it needs it.Safety: the chicken was already fully cooked, so it just needs warming. If you want it hotter, stir and leave the lid on for 2 minutes.
  7. Pour into 2 containers and let cool, then lid and fridge. Reheat in the microwave until piping hot. Keeps 3–4 days; also freezes very well.

Baked Banana Oat Bars

Oven does the workHands-on: ~12 minOven: 20–25 min

Kit you'll need

1 baking tray or dish (roughly A4-sized)1 large mixing bowlA fork (to mash banana)Baking paper

Ingredients

  • 3Ripe bananasThe browner the better — easier to mash and naturally sweeter
  • 200 gPorridge oatsAbout 2 mugs
  • 2Eggs
  • 1 scoopProtein powder (optional but recommended)Boosts the protein; vanilla or chocolate both work
  • 200 mlMilk
  • 2 tbspHoney
  • 1 tspCinnamon (optional)
  • 1 pinchSalt

Method

  1. Preheat the oven to 180°C (fan) / 200°C (no fan) / gas mark 6. Line your baking tray with baking paper (it stops the bars sticking).
  2. Mash the bananas. Peel them into the large bowl and mash with a fork until fairly smooth — a few lumps are fine.
  3. Crack in the 2 eggs and add the milk, honey, protein powder, cinnamon and pinch of salt. Stir well until combined.
  4. Add the oats and stir until everything is evenly mixed. It will be a thick, wet, spoonable batter — that's correct.
  5. Pour the mixture into the lined tray and spread it out evenly with the back of a spoon, so it's roughly the same thickness all over (about 2cm deep).
  6. Bake for 20–25 minutes, until the top is golden and firm to a light touch in the middle.How to check: press the centre gently — it should spring back rather than feel wet or sloppy. If unsure, give it another 3–4 minutes.
  7. Cool, then cut. Let it cool in the tray for 10–15 minutes, then lift it out by the paper and cut into 6 bars.
  8. Store: in a tub in the fridge for 4–5 days. Grab one each morning — they're good cold, or warmed for 20 seconds. They also freeze well wrapped individually.

Yoghurt Pot + Boiled Eggs

Only boiling requiredHands-on: ~10 min for bothThe easiest hot-protein breakfast

Kit you'll need

1 small saucepan2 pots/tubs with lidsA slotted spoon

Ingredients (for 2 breakfasts)

  • 4Eggs (2 per breakfast)
  • 500 gGreek yoghurt or Skyr250g per pot
  • 80 gGranolaKeep separate until eating so it stays crunchy
  • 2 handfulsBerries
  • 2 tspHoney
  • A sprinkleMixed seeds (optional)

Method

  1. Boil the eggs. Put the 4 eggs in the saucepan and cover with cold water from the tap (enough to sit about 2cm above the eggs). Put on a high heat.
  2. Time from boiling. When the water reaches a rolling boil (lots of bubbles), turn it down to a simmer and set a timer for 9 minutes for firm, hard-boiled eggs that are easy to take to work.Tip: 9 minutes gives a fully set yolk that travels well and won't be messy in your bag.
  3. Cool them. When the timer goes, lift the eggs out with a slotted spoon and put them in a bowl of cold water for a few minutes. This stops them cooking and makes them easier to peel later. Leave the shells on until you eat them — they keep better that way.
  4. Fill the pots. Spoon 250g of yoghurt or Skyr into each pot. Add a handful of berries, a teaspoon of honey, and a sprinkle of seeds. Lid on.
  5. Keep granola separate in a small bag or tub and add it at your desk so it stays crunchy.
  6. Store: pots and eggs keep 4 days in the fridge. Each breakfast is one pot plus 2 boiled eggs on the side — that's where the protein comes from.

Beef & Sweet Potato Chilli

Griddle/pan + rice cookerHands-on: ~20 minFreezes brilliantly

Kit you'll need

Blackstone griddle or a large frying pan / potRice cookerChopping board + knife3 lunch containers

Ingredients

  • 500 gBeef mince5% fat ("lean") is best for your goals
  • 1Sweet potato
  • 1 tinKidney beansDrained — tip into a sieve and rinse
  • 1 tinChopped tomatoes
  • 1Onion
  • 1Red pepper
  • 1–2 tspChilli powder1 for mild, 2 for more kick — your choice
  • 1 tsp eachCumin and smoked paprika
  • 1 tspGarlic (a jar is fine)
  • 200 gRice, uncookedFor the 3 portions
  • 1 tspOil for cooking

Method

  1. Start the rice in the rice cooker (200g rice, water to the line, switch on, leave it).
  2. Prepare the vegetables. Peel and finely chop the onion. Cut the pepper (remove the seedy core first) into small pieces. Peel the sweet potato and cut into roughly 1.5cm cubes.
  3. Brown the mince. Heat the oil on the griddle or in a large pot on medium-high heat. Add the beef mince. Break it up with a spoon and cook for about 6–8 minutes, stirring, until there's no pink left and it's lightly browned.Safety: mince must be cooked right through — no pink. Because it's been ground, any bacteria are mixed throughout, so thorough cooking matters.
  4. Add everything else: the onion, pepper, sweet potato, garlic, chilli powder, cumin and paprika. Stir for 2 minutes so the spices coat the meat.
  5. Add the tomatoes and beans. Tip in the tin of chopped tomatoes and the drained, rinsed kidney beans. Fill the empty tomato tin about halfway with water and add that too. Stir.
  6. Simmer. Turn the heat down so it bubbles gently, and let it cook for about 15 minutes, stirring now and then, until the sweet potato is soft (test with a fork) and the sauce has thickened. Taste and add a pinch of salt if needed.On a Blackstone: if your griddle can't hold liquid, just brown the mince on it, then move everything to a saucepan on the hob for the simmering stage.
  7. Build the 3 portions. Divide the rice between 3 containers and spoon the chilli on top. Let it cool.Important for rice: get the portions into the fridge within 1 hour of cooking — the same rule as any rice dish. Cool quickly, refrigerate promptly.
  8. Store: 3–4 days in the fridge (the rice is the limiter), or freeze for up to 3 months. Reheat until piping hot.

Chicken Pesto Pasta Pot

1 pan + griddleHands-on: ~15 minEaten cold — no reheating

Kit you'll need

1 saucepanBlackstone griddle or frying panA colander/sieve2 lunch containers

Ingredients

  • 160 gPasta, uncookedAny short shape — fusilli or penne work well. About 80g per portion
  • 250 gChicken breast
  • 3 tbspGreen pestoFrom a jar; check it's one you like as it carries the flavour
  • 1 tbspOlive oil
  • 1 handfulCherry tomatoes, halved
  • 2 handfulsSpinach or rocket
  • A pinchSalt and pepper

Method

  1. Cook the pasta. Fill the saucepan with water, add a pinch of salt, and bring to the boil. Add the pasta and cook for the time on the packet (usually 10–12 minutes), until soft but with a slight bite.
  2. Drain and cool. Tip the pasta into a colander in the sink and run cold water over it for a few seconds to cool it and stop it sticking. Leave it to drain.
  3. Cook the chicken. While the pasta cooks: cut the chicken into bite-sized chunks. Heat the olive oil on the griddle or in a pan on medium-high heat and cook for about 8–10 minutes, turning, until white all the way through.Safety check: cut the biggest piece — the inside must be white, never pink. Wash hands, board and knife after handling raw chicken.
  4. Mix it all together. In a big bowl, combine the cooled pasta, the cooked chicken, the pesto, the halved tomatoes and the spinach or rocket. Add a pinch of salt and pepper and stir until everything is coated in pesto.

Ride drink · 1 bottle · up to 60g carbs/hr

Everyday Ride Bottle

No cookingHands-on: ~3 minMake the night before

Ingredients (per 750ml bottle)

  • 750 mlWaterOne standard cycling bottle full
  • 45 gSugar (white table sugar) or honeyAbout 3 level tablespoons of sugar
  • ¼ tspTable saltA small pinch — this matters, it replaces what you sweat out
  • Juice of ½Lemon or lime (for flavour)

Method

  1. Put the sugar and salt into the bottle.
  2. Add a splash of warm water (about 100ml) and shake for 20 seconds — warm water dissolves the sugar far more easily than cold, so it doesn't sit gritty at the bottom.
  3. Squeeze in the lemon or lime juice, then top up to full with cold water.
  4. Shake well, put it in the fridge overnight. Grab it on your way out. Cold drinks are absorbed faster and taste better on the bike.

High-Carb Performance Bottle

No cookingHands-on: ~3 minFor your longer weekend rides

Ingredients (per 750ml bottle)

  • 750 mlWater
  • 40 gMaltodextrinA flavourless carb powder, bought online; about 3 tablespoons

Method

  1. Put the maltodextrin and fructose into the bottle. Maltodextrin can clump, so add a splash of warm water first and shake into a smooth liquid before adding more.Why two sugars?   2. Add the salt, the lemon juice and a splash of squash if you'd like more flavour.
  3. Top up to full with cold water, shake hard, and refrigerate overnight.

Ride food · makes ~12 cakes · freezes

Savoury Rice Cakes

Rice cooker + griddle/panHands-on: ~20 minSet overnight, then slice

Kit you'll need

Rice cooker1 frying panA baking tray or dish (roughly A4-sized)Cling film

Ingredients

  • 400 gSushi rice (short-grain), uncookedMust be short-grain "sushi" rice — it's sticky, which holds the cakes together. Normal rice won't work here.
  • 3Eggs
  • 3 rashersBacon
  • 2 tbspSoy sauce
  • 1 tbspSesame oil
  • 1 splashRice vinegar (optional)

Method

  1. Cook the sushi rice in the rice cooker following the cup-and-water lines as normal. Sushi rice comes out deliberately sticky — that's what you want.
  2. Cook the egg and bacon. Dice the bacon small. Heat the sesame oil in the frying pan on medium heat and cook the bacon for 4–5 minutes until done. Crack in the 3 eggs and scramble them (stir constantly with a spatula) until set, about 2 minutes. Turn off the heat.
  3. Mix everything while the rice is still warm (it sticks together best warm). In a big bowl, combine the cooked rice, the bacon and egg, the soy sauce, and the splash of vinegar. Stir well.
  4. Press into the tray. Line your baking tray with cling film (let it hang over the edges). Tip the rice mixture in and press it down firmly and evenly with the back of a spoon or your hand, until it's about 2–3cm thick and well compacted. The firmer you press, the better they hold.
  5. Chill to set. Cover with the overhanging cling film and put in the fridge for at least 2 hours, or overnight. It firms up into a sliceable slab.
  6. Slice and wrap. Lift the slab out using the cling film, and cut into roughly 12 rectangles. Wrap each one individually in cling film or foil — these are your pocket-sized ride snacks.
  7. Store: fridge for 3 days, or freeze. If frozen, take them out as you leave — they thaw in your jersey pocket within an hour or two and are perfect to eat mid-ride.
Ride food · makes ~10 cakes · freezes

Sweet Rice Cakes — Banana & Honey

Rice cooker onlyHands-on: ~12 minFor the later hours of a long ride

Why these

On rides over about three hours, savoury flavours start to win — but it works the other way too. After a couple of hours of nothing but sweet gels and drinks some riders crave the opposite, and others find a soft sweet bite goes down more easily late on when appetite fades. These are a gentler, less salty alternative to the savoury cakes, and there's no frying involved at all.

Kit you'll need

Rice cookerA baking tray or dish (roughly A4-sized)A fork (to mash banana)Cling film

Ingredients

  • 400 gSushi rice (short-grain), uncookedMust be the sticky short-grain "sushi" rice again — it's what holds the cakes together
  • 2Ripe bananasThe riper and browner, the sweeter and easier to mash
  • 3 tbspHoney (or maple syrup)
  • 1 tspCinnamon (optional)
  • 1 pinchSaltJust a small pinch — it lifts the flavour and replaces a little of what you sweat

Method

  1. Cook the sushi rice in the rice cooker following its cup-and-water lines, exactly as for the savoury version. It comes out sticky, which is what you want. Leave it to cool for 5 minutes so it's warm rather than piping hot.
  2. Mash the bananas. Peel them, put them in a big bowl, and mash with a fork until smooth — a few small lumps are fine.
  3. Mix everything together while the rice is still warm (it sticks best warm). Add the cooked rice, the honey, the cinnamon if using, and the pinch of salt to the mashed banana. Stir well until it's evenly combined.
  4. Press into the tray. Line the baking tray with cling film, letting it hang over the edges. Tip the mixture in and press it down firmly and evenly with the back of a spoon until it's about 2–3cm thick. The firmer you press, the better the cakes hold together.
  5. Chill to set. Cover with the overhanging cling film and refrigerate for at least 2 hours, or overnight, until it's firm enough to slice.
  6. Slice and wrap. Lift the slab out by the cling film and cut into roughly 10 rectangles. Wrap each individually in cling film or foil.
  7. Store: fridge for 3 days, or freeze and take out as you leave — they thaw in your pocket within an hour or two.Tip: carry a mix of sweet and savoury cakes on a long ride and alternate them. Swapping flavours stops "flavour fatigue", the point where everything tastes the same and you stop wanting to eat — which is exactly when you most need to.
Ride food · makes ~16 balls · no cooking

No-Bake Date & Oat Energy Balls

No oven, no hobHands-on: ~15 minA long-ride favourite

Why these

Energy balls are one of the most popular homemade ride foods going, for good reason: no cooking at all, they pack a lot of carbohydrate into a small bite, they don't melt or go to crumbs in a jersey pocket, and dates give natural fast-acting sugars. They're also the easiest recipe on this whole page — if you make nothing else, make these.

Kit you'll need

A food processor or blender1 mixing bowlA plate or tub to store them

No processor? You can still make them — see the note at the end.

Ingredients

  • 200 gMedjool dates, stones removedAbout 10–12 dates. Squeeze each one and pull out the hard stone in the middle — don't skip this
  • 200 gPorridge oatsAbout 2 mugs
  • 3 tbspPeanut butter (or any nut butter)Smooth is easier to mix than crunchy
  • 2 tbspHoney
  • 1 pinchSalt
  • 2 tbspCocoa powder or desiccated coconutOptional — for rolling the balls in at the end

Method

  1. Soften the dates. Put the pitted dates in a bowl, cover with warm water from the kettle, and leave for 10 minutes. This makes them soft enough to blend into a paste. Then drain off the water.
  2. Blend the dates. Put the softened, drained dates in the food processor and blitz for about 30 seconds until they form a sticky paste. Stop and scrape down the sides if they climb up.
  3. Add everything else — the oats, peanut butter, honey and pinch of salt — and blitz again for 20–30 seconds until it all comes together into a thick, sticky dough. It should hold its shape when you squeeze a bit in your hand. Too dry/crumbly? Add a teaspoon of honey or a splash of water and blitz again. Too sticky to handle? Add a spoonful more oats.
  4. Roll into balls. Scoop out heaped teaspoons of the mixture and roll each between your palms into a ball about the size of a walnut. You should get around 16. Tip: lightly wet your hands first and the mixture won't stick to them.
  5. (Optional) Coat them. Roll each ball in a little cocoa powder or desiccated coconut on a plate. This stops them sticking together and adds a bit of flavour.
  6. Chill to firm up. Put them on a plate or in a tub and refrigerate for at least 1 hour. They firm up and hold their shape much better once cold.
  7. Store: they keep in a tub in the fridge for up to 5 days. For the bike, wrap a few individually in cling film or pop them in a small sandwich bag.No food processor? Use ready-made date paste (sold in bags) or very finely chop soft dates by hand, then mix everything in a bowl with a sturdy spoon and your hands. It takes more elbow grease but works perfectly well.
Zero-effort ride fuel: if you'd rather not make any of these, shop-bought malt loaf (sliced and wrapped in foil the night before), fig rolls, Medjool dates and jelly sweets all work brilliantly on the bike with no preparation at all. They're already on your Lidl list.
