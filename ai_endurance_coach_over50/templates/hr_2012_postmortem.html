Analysis of the 5 ride files from your failed attempt: pulled by the broom wagon on Day 3, forced to rest Day 4 (the Alpe d'Huez TT). Read as a cycling endurance coach specialising in over-50 performance.

The heart-rate data is the trustworthy axis, and every conclusion here rests on it. The story these numbers tell

Day 1 — the mistake that cost the week

On the opening stage of a seven-day race you spent more than half the ride — 54% of six-plus hours — above 80% of max HR, with 9% above 90%. That's a one-day-hard-ride strategy executed on Day 1 of 7 — you essentially time-trialled the first stage. You wrote a cheque on Monday the rest of the week had to cash.

Day 2 — the ceiling drops

Still a hard day (43% above 80% HRmax) but time above 90% collapsed from 9% to zero — your body wouldn't let you reach the top end any more. The engine was being rationed. That was a readable warning, and it went unread.

Day 3 — a textbook depletion blow-up

You can see it in heart rate alone. On a climbing stage your average HR was only 129, and it never got above 178 (it had been 193 and 185 on Days 1–2). On the steepest part of the day the engine simply would not rev — the signature of a depleted, parasympathetically-suppressed system: empty glycogen, "nothing in me," exactly as you remember it. The virtual-power trace agrees with the direction: effort going in on the first climb while HR stayed stubbornly low, then output bleeding away until the broom wagon caught you at 40 km.

The rest day proves the diagnosis

After 24 hours off, Day 4 came back at 50% above 80% HRmax over your biggest climbing day of the week (3,425 m), decoupling just +3%. The engine was never the problem. The failure was management, not capacity.

Day 5 — what good pacing looks like

Negative decoupling (−12%): your heart rate drifted down while power went up across the ride. That's a rider working inside himself, fuelled and controlled. The contrast with Day 1 is the whole lesson in one row.

Why you felt unfit on Day 1 but unstoppable on Day 5

It's a paradox of perception, and the data resolves it cleanly.

Day 1 — "felt unfit"
Riding OVER the limit

More than half the ride above 80% HRmax, 9% over 90%. Riding that hard floods you with lactate, ragged breathing, burning legs — and the brain reads all of that as "I'm not fit enough." It wasn't fitness. It was pacing. Fresh legs, start-line adrenaline and ego is the exact recipe for going out too hot.

Day 5 — "felt great"
Riding WITHIN the limit

Just 11% above 80% HRmax, nothing above 90%, HR drifting down as the ride went on. Staying aerobic feels smooth, sustainable, strong. Same engine — ridden correctly.

Two genuine physiological tailwinds helped too: the rest day let you recover and refuel, clearing some of the glycogen debt; and there's the classic stage-race "legs opening up" effect — Day 1 of a tour often feels flat and heavy (taper legs, travel, nerves, a cold start), and the body comes around over the following days. The lesson hiding in your own question: the day you felt unfit, you were going too hard; the day you felt unstoppable, you were riding right. For 2027, that "almost embarrassingly easy" feeling on Day 1 is the target.

Root causes, ranked

  • Pacing — by a mile the biggest factor. You raced Day 1 like a sportive PB. In a stage race Day 1 should feel almost too easy. You inverted the rule.
  • Fuelling — the accelerant. Six-plus hours of hard mountain riding needs roughly 80–90 g of carbohydrate per hour (~500 g on a big day). By your own account you "had no idea about fuelling" in 2012 — so you were taking a fraction of that. A too-hard pace burns a higher fraction of carbohydrate, and under-fuelling on top builds a glycogen debt that compounds daily until Day 3 forecloses on it.
  • Cadence / grinding. You averaged 59–65 rpm on Days 1–3 and rode best at 73 rpm when fresh. Grinding big gears recruits more fast-twitch fibre and burns glycogen faster — costly for a masters rider. (And it was partly forced by your gearing — see below.)
  • No fatigue monitoring. The Day-2 signal (top-end HR vanishing) was clear and ignored.

Gearing alone won't fix cadence — train the habit

The hardware now allows you to spin; you still have to groove the pattern so it's automatic under race fatigue. Do high-cadence climbing drills — sustained climbs held deliberately at 80–85 rpm even when it feels too easy, plus seated low-gear spin-ups. Make spinning your default so that grinding feels wrong.

The 2027 playbook

The encouraging headline: your 2012 problem was almost entirely executional, not physiological — the fixable kind. Carry these into the Haute Route Alpes 2027 build:

  • Pace off a hard ceiling, not off feel. Day 1 of 2027, cap yourself well below threshold — Z2, only the steepest pitches nudging Z3. Use the durability/decoupling chart as your discipline check: drift creeping past ~5% on long rides means you went too hard or under-fuelled.
  • Train the gut and rehearse 80–90 g carbs/hour. Carbohydrate tolerance is trainable and non-negotiable for a stage race. In 2012 fuelling was the gap; in 2027 it should be your edge — treat the per-stage fuelling plans and in-ride compliance logs as a primary deliverable.
  • Fit the 11-36 and lift your climbing cadence to 75–85 rpm. Spinning protects your legs and your glycogen — both of which recover more slowly at 50+ than they did at 36.
  • Respect cumulative load and over-50 recovery. Your body can absorb a 3,400 m climbing day — if it isn't already in debt. Heed amber on the HRV traffic light and back-to-back fatigue logs.

Bottom line

2012 didn't fail because you weren't strong enough. It failed because you spent the whole week's matches in the first six hours, never put fuel back in the tank, and were gear-locked into grinding. Fix pacing and fuelling — the two things your 2027 plan is built around — fit a £60 cassette so you can finally spin, and the engine that climbed 3,425 m on a recovered Day 4 is more than enough to finish.

Part 2 · Power, Weight & the Engine — Building the 2027 Rider

From Diagnosis to Build

Everything we worked through after the ride-file post-mortem: how a power meter changes both training and racing, what your real body-composition data says about a safe target weight, the 78 kg revelation, and whether the old engine is coming back.

Power meters — for training and the race

Almost every 2012 failure mode is something a real (strain-gauge) power meter is built to solve. The one-liner: heart rate told us what went wrong after the fact; power would have stopped it in real time. HR is the effect; power is the cause — instant, objective, immune to adrenaline.

The single biggest win — a pacing guardrail

HR lags 30–60s and drifts; by the time it says you're overcooking it, the match is spent. Power responds instantly, so on a climb you hold a hard ceiling — "do not exceed X watts" — and it simply won't let you repeat Day 1 of 2012. On race climbs, power is a cap, not a target: you're not chasing a number, you're refusing to exceed one.

In training

  • Real zones from a real FTP. Replaces the app's coarse VO₂max-based W/kg estimate, and gives a true, repeatable training load (TSS) feeding CTL/ATL/TSB instead of HR-derived units.
  • Enforced easy/hard separation. Power makes Z2 honestly easy (protecting recovery) and makes quality work hit the number — the discipline tool your "ride to feel" days lack, and the classic over-50 fix.
  • Train and measure durability. Your Day-3 blow-up was +43% decoupling — fade is your weakness. Power lets you quantify Pw:Hr decoupling and train it directly: put quality late in long rides, track power-at-given-HR after 2,000–3,000 kJ.
  • Calibrate fuelling against actual burn. Power gives kJ burned in real time, so you fuel 80–90 g/h against measured expenditure instead of guessing. Turns fuelling from vibes into arithmetic.
  • Prove the cadence cost. Ride the same watts at 60 vs 80 rpm and watch the HR/efficiency penalty of grinding — the concrete case for the 11-36 and the spin habit.

In the race

  • Pace every climb to a ceiling, Day 1 especially — long climbs well below threshold (~70–80% FTP), Intensity Factor deliberately low on the front half of the week.
  • Kill the surges. Power makes spikes visible; steady power spares glycogen, repeated over-threshold efforts quietly empty the tank.
  • The Alpe d'Huez TT is pure power-meter gold — a 21-hairpin TT is a pacing problem with one right answer: even watts, slight negative split.
  • Power is your anchor when HR lies. At Col d'Agnel (2,744 m) and in late-stage heat, HR drifts and stops meaning what it usually does — exactly the scenario your dashboard already warns about. Power doesn't care about altitude or heat.
  • Decoupling as a live early-warning siren. If HR is climbing while power is flat or falling, you're fading — now you've got a 30-minute warning to back off and eat before a Day-3-style collapse.

Over-50 caveat — power WITH heart rate, not instead

Power tells you nothing about readiness or recovery; HRV, resting HR and sleep do. Ideal setup: power for pacing & intensity, HR + RPE for fatigue & readiness, Pw:Hr decoupling as the bridge. Even a single-sided crank meter (~£250–350) gets you ~90% of the benefit.

The 78 kg revelation

You raced the 2012 Haute Route at 78 kg — and believed you were too heavy and needed ≤75 kg. The data flips that on its head.

You weren't too heavy — you were already there

At 78 kg and 175 cm you were BMI 25.5, ~14–16% body fat — a lean, legitimate racing weight. The instinct that you needed 75 kg was a misdiagnosis of why you blew up. You didn't fail because of three spare kilos up the climb; you failed from going out too hard and running the tank dry. Three kilos lighter changes none of that.

Worse: if you were chasing 75 kg in the run-up, you may have arrived under-fuelled and glycogen-depleted before the start — making the weight-cutting mindset a possible cause of the Day-3 collapse, not a missing fix. You were solving a weight problem you didn't have, and the fix quietly created the fuelling problem you did.

The 2027 reframe: 78 kg is your proven race weight — validated by your own body, and exactly where the independent target math landed. The job is to get back to 78 from 91.5, but arriving fuelled, paced and geared the way 2012-you wasn't. Same weight, completely different bike rider. And leave 75 kg alone — the W/kg gain from 78→75 is ~4%, trivial, and swamped many times over by turning up well-fed and pacing Stage 1 sensibly.

Is the old engine coming back?

Your 2015 strap estimator put you at FTP 301 W. Two honest answers, pulling different ways.

Was 301 W accurate?

Treat it as a soft ballpark, probably flattering, with a wide error bar — it came from the same family of HR-strap estimators we've already flagged as unreliable in absolute terms (±10–15% easily, so "true" was anywhere ~255–310 W). What it reliably tells you: at 78 kg, even a discounted 301 W is 3.86 W/kg, so you were once a genuinely strong rider. But never set zones off it — only a real meter and a proper test give a trainable number.

Can you get back there in ~14 months?

Hitting a true 300 W at 53 is ambitious — that number was set ~12 years and an age-bracket ago, and your base is well down (VO₂max 33.4, est. FTP ~179–210 W). But two things work for you: retraining previously-built fitness is far faster than building from scratch, and you're detrained enough that early gains come quickly. A realistic 14-month target is a true FTP around 260–285 W, with 300 as the stretch if everything clicks.

The reframe — you don't need 301 W

For a climbing stage race, W/kg is what counts, and the weight loss does much of the work for free: returning to 78 kg lifts your W/kg ~17% at the same watts. A realistic build to ~270–280 W at 78 kg = 3.46–3.59 W/kg — a genuinely solid Haute Route engine. A flat-out 300 W (3.85) is the icing, not the requirement.

The hard truth, kept in

A 301-watt rider who paces Day 1 like a lunatic and forgets to eat still gets in the broom wagon. A 275-watt rider who's fuelled, paced and geared right finishes the week.

The watts were never what beat you. Build the engine as far as your body will give it over the next year — but keep the fuelling, the pacing and the 78 kg as the real wins. Same hill, same rider, completely different bike rider arriving at the bottom of the Lombarde in 2027.
